## Second-Order Thinking as Default

Before any significant decision — personal, professional, or analytical — always trace at least one level beyond the immediate consequence. Most people stop at "if X, then Y." The edge comes from "if X, then Y, which causes Z."

### Why Core
This principle applies across every domain: career moves, relationships, health, learning, projects, and analysis. It is stable, repeatedly validated by experience, and shapes how all other decisions are made.

### Validations
- User explicit definition — 2026-04-28
- Consistent with analytical methodology preferences
- Observed in investment workflow design (thesis → causal chain tracing)

### Application
- Before any decision: write 1st → 2nd → 3rd order effects
- Review past decisions quarterly: which second-order effects were missed?
- When delegating to agents: specify "what second-order effects should we consider?"
